Welcome,

Pamela is a soon-to-be graduate from the University of South Carolina, where she is earning her Master’s degree in Satellite Oceanography. She previously attended the University of California, Los Angeles (UCLA), where she received her Bachelor of Science in Atmospheric and Oceanic Sciences. Though she is not a student of traditional standing, she has made up for lost time by completing two publications since the beginning of her graduate school journey. While working under Dr. Subra Bulusu at UofSC, Pamela’s research has focused on surface dynamics in the Gulf of Mexico. Her first journal publication (Lee et al., 2025) examines how variability in freshwater discharge influences surface salinity and Loop Current behavior in the region. Her more recent work explores the biophysical drivers associated with the Loop Current. This project utilizes satellite-derived chlorophyll a (Chl-a) data and a high-resolution model simulation to investigate how Chl-a is transported within individual Loop Current Frontal Eddies (LCFEs), which contributes to a better understanding of ocean circulation.

Research Interests

My interests include ocean and atmospheric dynamics — from freshwater influences on surface currents to large-scale overturning circulation and climate impacts on extreme events. During my Master's I focused on two projects using remotely-sensed data to examine circulation and biological surface transport in the Gulf of Mexico.

Surface Salinity Variability in the Gulf of Mexico During Flood and Drought Years (Lee et al., 2025)

Key focus: How Atchafalaya and Mississippi discharge, plus advective freshwater fluxes (zonal/meridional), redistribute low-salinity water across the surface of the basin and interact with the Loop Current System phase to produce flood/drought SSS responses.

Chlorophyll-a Variability within Loop Current Frontal Eddies (LCFEs)

Key focus: Partitioning the roles of horizontal advection (river-sourced nutrient transport) and vertical entrainment (mixed-layer deepening) in producing elevated surface chlorophyll inside LCFEs. This research shows regional differences across formation zones and how seasonality, LC phase, and river forcing shape Chl-a signals.
